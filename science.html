1) Introduction and Problem Statement

Today's universities are considered highly desirable places to be, but the pressures they place on students and on their mental health are substantial. Mental health among students these days is generally poor, with high rates of anxiety, depression, sleep disorders, and other detrimental conditions afflicting today's college students.'[A] Not only do these conditions create subjective distress and misery, they also can seriously hinder academic performance, potentially causing career setbacks for years to come. Many mental health resources exist to deal with these issues, however they are not universally available due to financial constraints such as availability of health insurance, lack of time, or difficulty procuring transportation. Psychotherapy requires prolonged committment to treatment and financial resources, while medication often produces intolerable side-effects. If an intervention could be designed that was low-cost or free, personalizable, and effortlessly deployable alongside a student's pre-existing lifestyle that produced any non-trivial improvement in mental health symptoms or academic performance, that would be a significant finding in terms of population health. Due to their status as potential future leaders of society, college students are in a unique position to disproportionately impact the world relative to their numbers. Targeting a near-effortless but potentially impactful intervention towards them has the potential to spread benefits both forwards through their future careers and out into society as their cultural influence spreads into non-college educucated populations.

2) Binaural Beats Background

Binaural beats are an auditory illusion perceived when two slightly different frequency tones are presented separately, one to each ear. The brain processes these two signals and perceives a third, phantom beat at the difference between the two frequencies. For example, presenting a 200 Hz tone to the left ear and a 210 Hz tone to the right ear will result in the perception of a 10 Hz binaural beat with a perceived carrier wave frequency of 205 Hz. The theory of brainwave entrainment suggests that exposure to these frequencies can encourage the brain to synchronize its own electrical cycles to the frequency of the beat.2 Different brainwave frequencies are associated with different states of consciousness, although there is not exact consensus about these frequency ranges and boundaries.[B]

While research into the clinical efficacy of binaural beats is ongoing and presents mixed results, numerous studies have indicated their potential for reducing anxiety, improving sleep quality, and enhancing cognitive performance on certain tasks.1 Similarly, broadband noise, or "white noise," has long been used to mask distracting environmental sounds.[B] Different "colors" of noise, such as pink or brown noise, have different spectral properties that some may users find more pleasant or effective for relaxation and focus.[B] Traditional use of binaural beats in various forms of therapy has involved presentation of binaural beats (and accompanying noise-phenomena) with a fixed musical composition or other masking sounds for either technical reasons or, more recently, for convenience. Giving the users control over the binaural beat and structured noise frequencies, patterns, and volumes will allow them to tailor the therapeutic sound frequencies to best meet their current emotional and performance needs.

3) College Students and Binaural Beats

Students at modern universities experience intense pressure to succeed academically and socially. Many college students suffer from anxiety, acute or chronic stress, sleep problems, depression, or other mental health conditions.[A] Binaural beats accompanied by masking sounds are a promising potential therapeutic tool becuase they are effectively free (free sources as well as mainstream internet piracy), distributed effortlessly, and generally considered safe.The primary factor weighted against use of binaural beats as a treatment is the lack of a profit model that would encourage pharmaceutical or healthcare giants to spend the sums necessary to establish well-recognized standards and effect sizes in this field. 1,2 Given the lack of effective standard practices for binaural beats, this project and web-app offer an opportunity to users to customize their own experience with binaural beats, choosing their own carrier frequency, binaural beat frequency, and masker sounds as desired. With precise volume control, the users can use other audio on their computer with binaural beats overlaid at any volume, or even have multiple windows of the webapp open at once to layer multiple binaural beats into one signal (as in Lane & Kasian (1998)).

Important: Nothing on this page constitutes medical advice. If you are struggling with mental health or sleep, consider seeking professional support. Remember, binaural beats work best when they are barely noticeable, so keep the volume low to preserve efficacy and your hearing.

4) Proposed Mechanisms (What Might Be Happening)

4.1 Brainwave entrainment / changes in oscillatory activity

Brain activity at large scales is characterized by dominant oscillatory patterns, or "brain waves". Brain waves in different bands are always going through the brain while the brain is alive, however the prominence of a given brain wave typically reflects what the brain is doing or experiencing. One proposed mechanism for the effects of binaural beat exposure is that binaural beat stimulation can induce a frequency following response in the brain, leading to the dominant brain patterns experiencing "entrainment", where overall brain wave patterns begin to match the signal coming in through the auditory cortex. 6) What Seems to Matter (Why Results Differ)

  • Wear headphones: although some providers claim binaural beat induction using speakers, this contradicts the known mechanisms of binaural beat induction.
  • Beat frequency range: different beat frequencies produce different results
  • Carrier frequency: binaural beats are not perceived equally across carrier frequencies.
  • Exposure duration: Effects from single usages vs. habitual use are likely different.
  • Control condition quality: ensuring strong control conditions is challenging for an intervention where you can clearly hear the difference between conditions without careful work by the investigators.
  • Individual differences: in the brains and attributes of participants could affect outcomes
  • Masking noise/music context: different kinds of maskers or musical content can produce radically different results.

7) Practical Use and Safety Notes

  • Use headphones for binaural beats.
  • Keep volume low; effects are strongest when “barely perceptible,” , and loud stimulation can cause hearing damage.
  • Do not use while driving due to potential effects reducing arousal/awareness.
  • If you feel headache, discomfort, anxiety, or irritation, stop and reduce volume or try a different frequency.
  • This webapp was created for purposes of self-experimentation, not as a substitute for clinical care.

8) Future Directions

  • More and larger studies with adequate controls (which can be challenging).
  • Better standardization of binaural beat frequencies and carriers for given uses.
  • Identification of factors affecting who benefits most from various uses of binaural beats and why.
  • Broad studies of real-life use of binaural beats for therapeutic purposes in natural environments .
